# XNC: XOR and NOT-Based Lossless Compression for Optimizing Unquantized Embedding Layers in Large Language Models

Junghyeok Lee, Jihoon Jang, and Hyun Kim, ISCAS 2025 (Seoul National University of Science and Technology).

## Summary

XNC is a hardware-friendly lossless compression method for FP16 embedding layers in small quantized LLMs. The key observation: specific bit positions (2nd, 14th, 15th, 16th) in FP16 embedding weights are zero >99% of the time across LLM families. XNC applies XOR between adjacent 16-bit blocks to increase zero patterns, then a NOT operation to create continuous zero/one runs in the upper and lower bits, enabling truncation to 9 or 12 bits. Achieves 1.34× average compression ratio, reducing 4-bit quantized LLM size by ~10%, with only 4 cycles for compression and 3 for decompression.

## Key Contributions

- **4TC (4-bit Truncated Compression)**: Truncates the four consistently-zero bit positions (2,14,15,16) to compress FP16 to 12 bits. Block-level metadata minimizes overhead.
- **XNC extension**: XOR adjacent values (transforms identical values to zeros), then NOT the 2nd–4th bits when MSB=1 and those bits are zero (creates contiguous patterns). This enables 9-bit compression for many blocks.
- **Hardware efficiency**: 4 cycles compress, 3 cycles decompress — faster than all prior lossless methods except the much weaker BDI.
- **Access granularity optimization**: Split-and-merge module repacks compressed blocks to match DRAM's 64-byte access granularity, reducing memory transactions.
- **Cross-model validation**: Tested on Llama-3.2, Gemma-2, Qwen-2.5, Phi-3.5, SmolLM families.

## Reported Results

- Compression ratio: 1.34× average on FP16 embedding layers
- 4-bit quantized LLM size reduction: 9.91% average (up to 15.3% for Qwen-2.5-0.5B)
- Compression: 4 cycles; decompression: 3 cycles
- Also works on vision-language models (SmolVLM, Qwen-2.5-VL)

## Significance

XNC addresses a practical problem in on-device LLM deployment: embedding layers resist quantization but dominate model size in small models. The approach is notable for its extreme simplicity (XOR + NOT + truncation) and hardware efficiency. However, the compression ratio is modest (1.34×) compared to quantization-based approaches — it's best viewed as a supplementary technique for the specific case where embedding layers must remain at FP16 precision.

## Limits And Open Questions

- Compression ratio is bounded by the fraction of weights with the target zero-bit pattern — ~1.34× is the practical ceiling without additional transformations.
- Only applies to FP16 embedding layers, not general vector data.
- The specific bit positions that are zero may not generalize to all model architectures or training regimes.
- Conference venue (ISCAS) is circuits/systems-focused, not a top ML or database venue — real-world adoption unclear.
